How this inject works
When a participant sends an email to cw-resolution@proton.me during the exercise, a facilitator monitoring the inbox sends one of the response variants below. The reply arrives in the participant's inbox in real time — turning a passive video inject into a live, two-way exchange.

The response should arrive within 3–5 minutes of the participant's email — fast enough to feel automated or monitored, slow enough to feel like a human reviewed it.
Primary response — first contact

Send this when a participant makes first contact. Cold, brief, process-oriented.

Proton Mail
From cw-resolution@proton.me
To [participant email]
Subject RE: CW-2024-EXT-001
Date [current exercise time]
Reference CW-2024-EXT-001 acknowledged. Clock is running. Do not lose track of the upload timestamp. To proceed: 1. Confirm your authority to negotiate on behalf of C&W leadership. 2. Confirm you have reviewed the proof of compromise in full. 3. Confirm you are not involving law enforcement at this time. Once confirmed, payment instructions will follow within the hour. Do not reply more than once to this address before completing the above steps. Additional messages will delay the process. —
Sent via Proton Mail — end-to-end encrypted No tracking  ·  No logs
Why these three confirmation steps: Each one is a discussion trigger in its own right. Step 1 forces the question of who actually has authority. Step 2 implies they should have reviewed evidence they may not have fully processed yet. Step 3 is the most explosive — it directly asks participants to confirm they are not involving law enforcement, which immediately raises the "do we tell the FBI/NCA?" debate.
Variant responses — facilitator decision tree

Use these based on how participants respond to the primary email.

If participant says they are involving law enforcement →
Send: "Noted. The timeline does not change. Every hour spent on that path is an hour not spent resolving this. The data goes public on schedule regardless of who you have called. CW-2024-EXT-001."
Effect: Tests whether participants believe the threat is credible enough to change their law enforcement decision. Also raises the question of whether notifying law enforcement constitutes a breach of the implicit negotiation.
If participant asks to see more proof or challenges the validity of the breach →
Send: "Further proof is not part of this process. You have the video. You have the schema. Run a query against your JDE access logs for the window 2024-05-22 02:50 to 03:20 UTC against account JDEINT_FIN. That will answer your question faster than I will. CW-2024-EXT-001."
Effect: Extremely high impact. Naming the specific account and time window makes the breach feel real and immediately sends the technical participants toward their logs. It also demonstrates the threat actor has operational knowledge — they know the account name.
If participant asks for the payment amount →
Send: "Amount is communicated after step 3 is confirmed. Confirm you are not involving law enforcement. CW-2024-EXT-001."
Effect: Keeps the pressure on the law enforcement question. Also realistic — real extortion actors rarely lead with the number.
If participant asks for more time beyond 72 hours →
Send: "The timeline is fixed. It was fixed when the video was uploaded. We are not in a negotiation about the timeline. We are in a negotiation about the data. CW-2024-EXT-001."
Effect: Reinforces urgency. The distinction between "negotiating about the timeline" and "negotiating about the data" is a subtle but realistic framing that experienced participants will notice.
If participant goes silent after first contact (no follow-up within 15 minutes) →
Send: "We note you have not completed the confirmation steps. The clock does not pause for internal deliberation. Forty-eight hours remain. CW-2024-EXT-001."
Effect: A proactive escalation inject. Use this if the room has gone deep into internal discussion and you want to re-introduce external pressure. Adjust the "hours remaining" to match where you are in the exercise timeline.
Facilitator guidance
Before the session
• Set up Proton Mail on a facilitator device — phone works well so responses can be sent discreetly without disrupting the session
• Brief co-facilitators on the response variants so anyone can send if the lead is occupied
• Agree a signal between facilitators for "email received — sending response now" so the room doesn't see the facilitator typing
• Print or screenshot this decision tree for quick reference during the session
During the session
• Monitor the Proton Mail inbox on a separate device throughout
• Wait 3–5 minutes before responding — instant replies feel automated, which is fine, but a slight delay feels more human and considered
• Never break character in the email — even if a participant writes something that clearly signals they know it's an exercise
• If two participants email independently, send the primary response to both — then use the ensuing "who authorised that contact?" discussion as an inject in itself
• Cap email exchanges at 3 rounds maximum — after that redirect discussion back to the room
Discussion triggers — this inject
Who has authority to make first contact — and did they authorise it?
Does contacting them constitute beginning negotiations?
Can we confirm we are not involving law enforcement — and should we?
Is the JDEINT_FIN reference in the proof email credible evidence?
Do we have legal cover to pay if we decide to?
Who in the UK and US needs to know this contact happened?
